Game-facing backend work often begins with something that looks small:
authentication, session state, progression sync, entitlement checks.

The mistake is to treat each of those as isolated plumbing. They are usually the
start of a platform shape, whether the team acknowledges it or not.

The reason I find backend service prototypes valuable is not because they are
finished products. It is because they force the right architectural questions
early:

- What belongs in identity, and what belongs in player state?
- What is shared across titles, and what should stay game-specific?
- How do you keep transport and domain decisions separate enough to evolve?

Even a small prototype can answer those questions better than a loose set of
helpers spread across a codebase.

That is why I like service-oriented experiments that stay narrow but intentional.
They create reusable thinking before they create reusable code.
